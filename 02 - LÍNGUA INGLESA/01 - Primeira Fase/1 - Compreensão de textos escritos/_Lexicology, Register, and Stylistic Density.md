# Note 1 — Academic and Diplomatic Vocabulary: Lexicology, Register, and Stylistic Density for the CACD

## Block 1 — Stylistic Debate and Language Register (Historiography of Discourse)

The preparation for the _Concurso de Admissão à Carreira de Diplomata_ (CACD), administered by the Instituto Rio Branco (IRBr) and evaluated by Cebraspe, demands an exceptionally sophisticated command of the English language. Candidates must transcend basic fluency to achieve a level of lexical precision and stylistic awareness that mirrors professional diplomatic discourse. This block explores the foundational stylistic debates that shape modern international relations literature, providing the theoretical grounding necessary for mastering the advanced vocabulary required in both the objective (TPS) and discursive (Composition and Translation) phases of the examination.

## The Tension Between Plain English and Classical Formality

The evolution of academic and diplomatic writing in English is fundamentally characterized by an enduring, unresolved tension between the **Plain English** movement and the entrenched tradition of utilizing **Hellenisms** and **Latinisms**. The push for linguistic clarity and objectivity is most famously championed by George Orwell in his seminal 1946 essay, _Politics and the English Language_. Orwell vehemently criticized the reliance on dead metaphors, foreign phrasing, and excessive circumlocution, arguing that such convoluted language is primarily used to defend the indefensible and obfuscate political reality. In the modern era, this advocacy has manifested in widespread governmental mandates to utilize plain language, aiming to democratize access to legal and scientific information without "dumbing down" the underlying complexities.

> [!info] Historiografia 
> The contemporary critique of dense academic writing often positions Orwell's principles as the ultimate benchmark for linguistic propriety. However, modern historiographical and linguistic analysis reveals that the purist approach of the Plain Language Movement frequently fails to account for the highly pragmatic and performative nature of diplomatic English. Language in the realm of statecraft is not merely a vehicle for the transmission of raw data; it is a meticulously ritualized form of geopolitical interaction. As noted by analysts of historical rhetoric, the deliberate use of awkward subordinate clauses, calculated circumlocutions, and elevated classical terminology functions as the "body language" of international negotiations, providing essential tonal cues that straightforward, blunt language inherently lacks. Furthermore, historical analyses of major international legal instruments, such as United States nuclear treaties (e.g., the Non-Proliferation Treaty and the Strategic Arms Reduction Treaties), demonstrate that while there is a gradual trend toward structural simplification, the core diplomatic vocabulary remains highly formalized. This resistance to total linguistic simplification is not merely professional vanity; it preserves long-standing interpretative precedents and avoids the diplomatic friction that sudden shifts in standard terminology might provoke.

Despite the undeniable value of clarity, Hellenisms and Latinisms perform a vital gatekeeping and standardizing function within international law and diplomacy. Legal scholars and historians observe that nonessential Latin terms are not only holding steady but frequently gaining traction in high-level judicial opinions and diplomatic communiqués. For instance, a diplomatic text will deliberately employ classical derivatives to elevate the register, conveying an institutional gravitas that grounds contemporary disputes in historical precedent. The use of an elevated register provides a precise, universally recognized conceptual shorthand that transcends the fluid, often ambiguous nature of conversational English.

For the CACD candidate, this dichotomy presents a strategic challenge. In the objective phase (TPS), Cebraspe tests the candidate's passive recognition of these classical borrowings, demanding exact semantic precision to navigate complex reading comprehension passages. In the discursive phases, the candidate must deploy this elevated register with extreme tactical awareness. Overusing Latinisms can plunge a text into pedantry, violating the modern expectation of analytical clarity; however, stripping a diplomatic composition of its traditional classical underpinnings reduces its stylistic elevation, causing the text to read like a journalistic summary rather than a sophisticated state paper. The mastery of register lies in the judicious interpolation of classical vocabulary within clean, modern syntactic structures.

## The Rhetorical Function of Dense Literary Prose

In academic literature pertaining to International Relations (IR), **dense literary prose** serves a profound rhetorical function: it transforms abstract geopolitical theories and structural analyses into visceral, memorable paradigms. The most effective strategic thinkers and diplomats do not rely exclusively on sterile, quantitative assessments; they employ evocative imagery, historical allegories, and sophisticated syntax to shape the cognitive framework of their audience.

The challenge for the diplomatic candidate lies in harnessing this descriptive power without descending into **purple prose**—writing that is so overly ornate, melodramatic, or adjectivally saturated that it distracts from the analytical core of the argument.

> [!info] Historiografia 
> The rhetorical mastery of twentieth-century diplomats like George F. Kennan and Henry Kissinger exemplifies the ideal balance between analytical rigor and literary density. Kennan, who formulated the foundational Cold War strategy of containment, was often noted by his contemporaries, including Dean Acheson, to be as significant a literary figure as a policy maker. Kennan’s prose was deeply introspective, utilizing complex, layered imagery to convey the psychological and historical dimensions of Soviet behavior. Similarly, Kissinger’s writings are frequently characterized by an "elegiac tone" and profound philosophical reflections on the tension between American idealism and realpolitik. By employing a sophisticated, slightly archaic lexicon, these statesmen endowed their policy prescriptions with the weight of historical inevitability. Their writing demonstrates that in high diplomacy, the aesthetic quality of the argument often legitimizes its strategic content.

When drafting a composition for the CACD, the deployment of dense literary vocabulary—words that evoke movement, texture, and emotional resonance—allows the candidate to construct strong, authoritative imagery. Describing a failed multilateral negotiation not merely as a "failure," but as leaving behind a landscape of diplomatic wreckage, immediately elevates the text. It signals to the examiner that the candidate possesses native-like command over the language's associative and connotative dimensions. However, this technique requires severe syntactic discipline. The surrounding grammatical structure must remain crystalline and logical. The juxtaposition of a highly evocative, literary noun or verb within a strict, analytically precise sentence structure is the hallmark of advanced academic English. It creates a rhythm that captivates the reader, fulfilling the dual requirement of stylistic elevation and objective clarity.

## The Impact of Derivational Morphology on Discourse Flexibility

**Derivational morphology**—the linguistic process of forming a new word from an existing base through the addition of **affixes** (prefixes and suffixes)—is a critical engine of lexical flexibility in academic and diplomatic discourse. A nuanced understanding of morphology extends far beyond the rote memorization of vocabulary lists; it empowers the writer to manipulate the tone, register, and ideological framing of a text dynamically.

In political and academic writing, the spontaneous creation of **neologisms** or the unconventional attachment of suffixes can instantly shift the tone of a passage from strictly analytical to subtly ironic, skeptical, or polemical. Suffixation is not merely a grammatical mechanical operation; it carries significant semantic weight and pragmatic intention.

> [!info] Historiografia 
> Recent linguistic studies analyzing political and internet discourse highlight how derivational morphology is frequently weaponized to express hyperbole, sarcasm, and irony. The use of culturally specific pseudo-suffixes or the intentional distortion of word bases serves to mock and delegitimize political opponents or bureaucratic processes. For example, the creation of morphologically hybridized terms in conflict zones demonstrates how language adapts to convey a mocking-ironic tone. In high-level academic prose, this mechanism is utilized more subtly. A scholar might employ a highly technical, Latinate suffix on a colloquial base, or conversely, attach an informal suffix to a rigorous academic concept, to satirize bureaucratic overreach. This establishes an ironic distance between the author and the subject matter, signaling to the reader a critical or skeptical stance without overtly violating the formal constraints of the academic register.

For the CACD candidate, mastering these morphological rules is indispensable across all phases of the examination. In the TPS, recognizing how a specific suffix alters both the syntactic category and the subtle semantic shade of a root word is essential for decoding the underlying attitude of the author in complex reading comprehension passages. In the written phases, the flawless execution of hyphenation rules when attaching prefixes and suffixes demonstrates a meticulous command of standard editorial practices. The ability to correctly apply rules regarding double consonants, compound modifiers, and the divergences between American and British English spelling conventions separates a competent English speaker from a master of the diplomatic register.

## Hellenisms and Latinisms

Classical borrowings are the bedrock of institutional English. They convey an implicit understanding of history, philosophy, and jurisprudence.

The term **hubris** originates from ancient Greek tragedy, denoting an exaggerated pride or self-confidence that inevitably provokes the wrath of the gods, resulting in fatal retribution (nemesis). In modern diplomatic and geopolitical analysis, it describes the dangerous overestimation of a state's own competence, military capabilities, or moral authority, often preceding a catastrophic foreign policy failure. It is frequently employed to critique unilateralism or imperial overstretch.

The word **aegis** is etymologically derived from the mythological shield or breastplate carried by Zeus or Athena, which offered divine protection. In contemporary international relations, it has evolved to signify the formal protection, backing, or sponsorship of a recognized multilateral organization. It confers a sense of legal and moral legitimacy upon an action or a treaty.

The adjective **apposite** is derived from the Latin _appositus_, meaning "put near" or "applied to." It is a highly elevated term used to describe a remark, quotation, or policy that is remarkably appropriate, pertinent, and perfectly suited to the specific circumstances being discussed. It is a marker of intellectual precision.

The phrase **noblesse oblige** is a French borrowing deeply entrenched in English diplomatic and socio-economic discourse. Literally translating to "nobility obliges," it implies that wealth, power, and prestige come with inherent responsibilities to those who are less fortunate. In the context of international political economy, it summarizes the moral economy wherein the privilege of hegemonic powers must be balanced by the duty to provide global public goods, such as Official Development Assistance (ODA) or security guarantees. It can, however, carry a pejorative undertone of paternalism or condescension, reflecting the unequal power dynamics between the global North and South.

|**aegis**|The protection, backing, or support of a particular person or organization; sponsorship.|_Égide, tutela, patrocínio, auspícios._|_Under the aegis of, to provide an aegis, a multilateral aegis._|

When deploying _noblesse oblige_ in translation exercises, recognize its dual nature—it is simultaneously a noble moral duty and a stark reminder of hierarchical inequality.

## Diplomatic Vocabulary

The structural vocabulary of international relations relies on terms that denote scope, foundational principles, and conditions of application.

The prepositional phrase **under the aegis of** is indispensable in legal and institutional texts. It is the standard diplomatic collocation used to describe the creation of treaties, the deployment of peacekeeping forces, or the establishment of international tribunals under the umbrella of a higher authority, such as the United Nations Security Council.

The compound adjective **far-reaching** is utilized to describe policies, agreements, or crises that possess an extensive scope and generate a wide array of implications across multiple sectors. It is a vital descriptive tool for discussing the cascading effects of macroeconomic reforms or global geopolitical shifts.

The noun **underpinnings** refers to the solid foundation or fundamental basis of a system. Unlike physical "foundations," _underpinnings_ in academic prose usually refer to the theoretical, ideological, or economic principles that support a political framework or international order. It is a term of profound structural analysis.

The prepositional phrase **irrespective of** is a critical cohesive device used to introduce a condition of universality or concession. It signifies that an action or rule applies without taking into account a specific, usually differentiating, factor. It is heavily utilized in human rights declarations and trade agreements to emphasize non-discrimination and uniform application.

|---|---|---|---|
|**under the aegis of**|Operating under the formal protection, sponsorship, or institutional framework of a recognized body.|_Sob a égide de, sob os auspícios de, no âmbito de._|_Under the aegis of the UN, established under the aegis of._|
|**far-reaching**|Having a wide range of influence, effect, or implications; extensive in scope.|_De longo alcance, de amplas consequências, abrangente, profundo._|_Far-reaching consequences, far-reaching reforms, far-reaching implications._|

> [!tip] Dica TPS

> In Phase 3 (Composition), elevating your vocabulary scores requires replacing common verbs and nouns with these precise diplomatic alternatives. Do not write that an agreement has "big effects"; write that it has **far-reaching implications**. Do not describe the "ideas behind the policy"; analyze its **theoretical underpinnings**. This demonstrates an internalized mastery of the academic register.

## Literary Vocabulary and Dense Prose

The incorporation of literary vocabulary into academic texts provides rhetorical force, utilizing metaphor and vivid imagery to convey complex political realities.

The noun **wreckage** describes the catastrophic remains of something destroyed. In IR literature, it is a powerful metaphor for the collapse of post-war orders, failed economic systems, or abandoned multilateral treaties, conveying a sense of profound, irreversible systemic failure.

The noun **drudgery** describes hard, menial, and exceptionally dull work. In diplomatic historiography, it frequently appears to describe the exhaustive, unglamorous reality of bureaucratic administration and the endless, meticulous drafting processes behind international agreements.

The verb **debouched** is a highly specific, low-frequency term originating from French military history, denoting troops emerging from a narrow, confined pass into a wide, open plain. By extension, in diplomatic prose, it is used metaphorically to describe a state or policy suddenly emerging from isolation onto the broad stage of global geopolitics.

The noun **wisp** denotes a small, thin, or twisted trace of something. It is utilized to inject delicate, ephemeral imagery into an analysis, often describing a fragile peace agreement, fading diplomatic leverage, or the last remnants of institutional trust.

The noun and verb **churn** originally referred to the violent agitation of making butter. In contemporary political economy and diplomatic analysis, it has been adopted to describe a state of constant, chaotic change, high turnover, or systemic instability, such as the volatility of emerging markets or turbulent electoral landscapes.

The adverb **willy-nilly** possesses a playful, reduplicative phonetic structure but carries a serious semantic weight. Originating from the archaic "will I, nill I," it indicates that an action occurs whether one likes it or not, often implying a state of disorganization, haphazardness, or compulsion against one's will.

The compound adjective **self-effacing** describes a character trait of modesty, indicating someone who does not claim attention for themselves and prefers to keep a low profile. It is frequently used in character studies of diplomats who excel in "quiet diplomacy" and behind-the-scenes, back-channel negotiations.

If a policy is described as being applied **willy-nilly**, the author is heavily criticizing its lack of strategic coherence.

## Derivational Morphology: Suffixes, Hyphenation, and Aglutination

A profound understanding of morphology and the specific editorial rules governing the attachment of affixes is a critical differentiator in the CACD, particularly in the written phases where orthographic precision is ruthlessly evaluated. Furthermore, variations between British and American English must be handled with absolute consistency.

The suffix **-ish** is used to form adjectives indicating an approximate age, time, or a vague quality (e.g., _childish_, _fifty-ish_). In academic prose, it is occasionally employed to inject an informal, skeptical, or slightly ironic tone into the analysis, undermining the precision of the noun it modifies. Editorially, it is generally attached directly to the base word without a hyphen. However, the cardinal rule of exception dictates that a hyphen must be used if the root word ends in the same vowel that begins the suffix (to avoid visual confusion and double vowels) or if it is being attached to a capitalized proper noun.

The suffix **-ness** is highly productive, used to form nouns expressing a state, quality, or condition (e.g., _usefulness_, _arbitrariness_). The primary orthographic shift occurs when the root ends in a consonant followed by a 'y'; the 'y' must be changed to an 'i' before adding the suffix (e.g., _happy_ becomes _happiness_). A frequent point of contention arises regarding the tripling of consonants. According to major style guides like the Chicago Manual of Style (CMS) and the Oxford Style Guide, hyphens are generally employed to avoid the tripling of identical consonants (e.g., _shell-like_, _hull-less_). However, when attaching _-ness_ to a word ending in a double 's' (e.g., _boss_), the resulting spelling is _bossness_ (double 's' followed by 'n', not a triple letter). But if the root word ends in an 'n' (e.g., _stubborn_), the double 'n' is retained (_stubbornness_).

The suffix **-ward** and its variant **-wards** are used to form adverbs and adjectives denoting spatial or temporal direction. This suffix represents one of the most critical and highly visible divergences between American and British English orthography.

|**Morphological Feature**|**Function and Semantic Nuance**|**Hyphenation and Editorial Rules**|**US vs. UK Divergences**|
|---|---|---|---|
|**-ish**|Forms adjectives denoting approximation or vague qualities. Creates a skeptical or ironic tone in formal writing.|Generally closed up. Hyphenate only if the base word ends in "i" or is a capitalized proper noun.|Generally uniform, though British English employs it more freely across diverse registers.|
|**-ness**|Forms abstract nouns denoting a state or quality.|Change consonant+'y' to 'i'. Retain double consonants of the root (e.g., _stubbornness_).|Uniform across varieties, but highly scrutinized for correct spelling of double consonants in written exams.|
|**-ward / -wards**|Forms adjectives and adverbs indicating direction.|Generally closed up without a hyphen (e.g., _eastward_, _upward_).|**Critical Difference:** American English uses **-ward** for both adjectives and adverbs. British English uses **-wards** for adverbs, but **-ward** for adjectives.|

> [!tip] Dica TPS In the Phase 3 Composition, you must explicitly choose either the American editorial standard (e.g., Chicago Manual of Style) or the British standard (e.g., Oxford Style Guide) and maintain it flawlessly throughout your essay. If you adopt British spelling for your vocabulary (writing _colour_, _defence_, and _analyse_), you **must** use **-wards** when forming adverbs of direction (e.g., "The negotiations moved _backwards_"). Conversely, if you write _color_ and _analyze_ (US), you must write "The negotiations moved _backward_". Mixing these conventions signals a severe lack of register control to the examining board. Note that the **Oxford spelling** standard uniquely dictates the use of the _-ize_ suffix for Greek-derived words (_organize_, _realize_) even within British English, a nuanced stylistic choice that demonstrates supreme academic sophistication if applied consistently.

## Block 3 — TPS Radar (Classic Pitfalls)

This section maps the specific cognitive, grammatical, and semantic traps laid by the Cebraspe examining board in the objective phase (TPS). The board relies heavily on false cognates, orthographic similarities, prepositional mismanagement, and subtle register errors to filter candidates. Mastery of these specific pitfalls is essential for achieving a competitive score.

> [!warning] Pegadinha 
> **The Orthographic and Semantic Confusion: Apposite vs. Opposite** Cebraspe frequently embeds the word **apposite** in highly dense reading comprehension texts to test the candidate's visual and semantic acuity. A candidate rushing through a complex passage under time pressure will often misread the term as _opposite_ (meaning contrary, facing, or conflicting) due to their near-identical orthography. _The Trap:_ A text might state: "During the summit, the Brazilian delegate made an apposite remark regarding tariff barriers." The subsequent True/False item will posit: "The text implies that the Brazilian delegate made a contradictory or hostile statement." _The Resolution:_ This is unequivocally **False**. To make an _apposite_ remark means the statement was perfectly fitting, highly relevant, and uniquely appropriate for the occasion. Conflating the two words completely inverses the comprehension of the character's diplomatic stance and ruins the analysis of the paragraph.

> [!warning] Pegadinha 
> **The Gross Prepositional Error: Irrespective of** In grammar-focused TPS questions, sentence completion exercises, and in the strict evaluation of the Phase 3 Composition, the phrase **irrespective of** is a prime target for prepositional errors. Due to linguistic interference from the Portuguese equivalent (_independentemente de_) and synonymous English phrases (_independent from_), candidates routinely write or accept the incorrect formulation _irrespective from_. _The Trap:_ The board will provide a complex sentence such as: "The multilateral treaty applies universally to all signatory member states, irrespective from their domestic macroeconomic policies or historical alignments." _The Resolution:_ This sentence is grammatically fatal. The phrase must strictly, without exception, be collocated as **irrespective of**. The board uses this specific error to instantly identify candidates whose English is heavily influenced by mother-tongue interference rather than native structural awareness.

> [!warning] Pegadinha 
> **The Semantic Misappropriation of Willy-Nilly** Candidates are often drawn to the adverb **willy-nilly** as an idiomatic flourish to spice up their discursive essays, incorrectly deploying it as a synonym for "willingly," "happily," or "voluntarily." This error stems from a fundamental misunderstanding of its etymology and phonetics. _The Trap:_ In a TPS reading text analyzing international political economy, a sentence might read: "Facing the sudden collapse of the global supply chain, the developing nations were dragged into the tariff war willy-nilly." The board will then offer an item stating: "According to the passage, the developing nations participated in the tariff war voluntarily." _The Resolution:_ This is **False**. _Willy-nilly_ derives from the archaic phrase "will I, nill I" (meaning whether I wish it or do not wish it). It signifies compulsion against one's will, or an action taken in a thoroughly disorganized, haphazard manner. Using it to mean "voluntarily" demonstrates a severe semantic inversion.

> [!warning] Pegadinha 
> **Register Errors in Translation: Noblesse Oblige and Hubris** When translating from English to Portuguese (Versão) or vice versa (Tradução), candidates frequently fail to match the stylistic register of the original text, resulting in heavy penalization. _The Trap:_ If a highly formal text detailing the historiography of the League of Nations uses the word **hubris** to describe institutional failure, translating it simply as _confiança_ (confidence) or _orgulho_ (pride) loses points for semantic flattening. _The Resolution:_ It must be translated using elevated terminology, such as _arrogância desmedida_ or the direct Portuguese borrowing _húbris_. Similarly, translating **noblesse oblige** in an academic text about the World Trade Organization simply as _obrigação dos ricos_ (the obligation of the rich) is far too colloquial and strips the phrase of its sociological weight. It must capture the historical power dynamic, translating ideally as _o dever inerente ao privilégio_ or retaining the French _noblesse oblige_ if italicized, as it is a widely recognized calque in Brazilian diplomatic literature and MRE documentation.

## Block 4 — Discursive Script (Phase 3/4 - Composition)

To succeed in the Phase 3/4 English Composition, the CACD candidate must synthesize absolute grammatical precision with immense lexical density. The objective is to produce a text that mirrors the structural and tonal expectations of a professional IR journal, reading like an editorial in _Foreign Affairs_ or a policy briefing from a premier think tank.

Below is an exhaustive argumentative skeleton for a composition regarding a contemporary geopolitical crisis—specifically, the breakdown of a multilateral environmental or non-proliferation treaty. This script explicitly integrates the advanced vocabulary analyzed in Block 2, providing syntactic strategies to maximize the stylistic score.

## Introduction: Presenting the Crisis and Diagnosing the Foundations

The introduction must establish the gravity and scope of the geopolitical crisis immediately, decisively bypassing generic hooks or historical clichés. The goal is to define the systemic failure and diagnose its root cause using elevated terminology, setting an authoritative academic tone from the first syllable.

**Strategic Vocabulary Implemented:** _Underpinnings_, _Hubris_.

**Syntactic Structure Strategy:** Employ a complex declarative sentence utilizing a nominalization as the subject, followed by a non-restrictive relative clause to add descriptive depth without fracturing the sentence's logical flow.

**Drafting Model:**

"The rapid and unchecked deterioration of the contemporary non-proliferation regime has laid bare the fragile **underpinnings** of global security architecture. Driven by the geopolitical **hubris** of revisionist state actors—who systematically overestimate their capacity to manage regional fallout—the recent unilateral withdrawals from multilateral frameworks threaten to dismantle decades of painstakingly constructed institutional trust. This essay argues that restoring systemic stability requires an immediate recommitment to collective security mechanisms, superseding narrow national interests."

**Analytical Insight for the Candidate:** By employing the term **underpinnings**, the candidate demonstrates a profound understanding of structural IR theory, looking past surface-level events to the foundational basis of the system. Framing the diplomatic failure as a direct result of **hubris** elevates the critique from a simple policy disagreement to a historical and behavioral indictment of the state actors involved, echoing the dense literary style of classical geopolitical analysis.

## Development: Comprehensive Consequences and the Imperative of Cooperation

The body paragraphs must meticulously map the cascading effects of the crisis and argue logically for an unconditional return to the negotiating table. This section must prove the candidate's ability to handle complex cause-and-effect relationships, manage advanced prepositional phrases, and deploy literary imagery without descending into purple prose.

**Strategic Vocabulary Implemented:** _Far-reaching_, _Irrespective of_, _Wreckage_, _Churn_.

**Syntactic Structure Strategy:** Use concessive subordinating conjunctions and introductory prepositional phrases to create a flowing, analytical rhythm. Employ fronting techniques to emphasize the scale of the problem.

**Drafting Model:**

"The consequences of this diplomatic rupture are undeniably **far-reaching**, extending far beyond immediate regional borders to destabilize global supply chains and established nuclear deterrence paradigms. Amidst the continuous political and economic **churn** that characterizes the current international system, states are forced to navigate through the **wreckage** of abandoned treaties in an attempt to forge new, albeit precarious, consensus. To arrest this decline, it is absolutely imperative that the international community enforces compliance mechanisms uniformly, **irrespective of** the historic alliances, the ideological alignment, or the asymmetric economic leverage wielded by the offending nations."

**Analytical Insight for the Candidate:** The metaphorical imagery of navigating through the **wreckage** amidst endless political **churn** provides the precise dense literary prose discussed in Block 1. It is highly evocative, painting a vivid picture of systemic instability, yet remains analytically grounded. The flawless grammatical deployment of **irrespective of** ensures maximum syntactic marks and establishes an objective, unwavering tone regarding the application of international law. The compound adjective **far-reaching** efficiently condenses the scope of the crisis, allowing the sentence to maintain momentum.

## Conclusion: Multilateral Solutions and the Responsibility of Major Powers

The conclusion must not merely summarize the preceding paragraphs; it should offer a strong normative prescription for the future. It must leverage diplomatic legacy terms to frame the proposed solution, leaving the examiner with a lasting impression of the candidate's strategic maturity.

**Strategic Vocabulary Implemented:** _Under the aegis of_, _Noblesse oblige_.

**Syntactic Structure Strategy:** Utilize a conditional structure or a strong modal verb phrasing to project authority and finality. Employ parallel structures to balance the final argument.

**Drafting Model:**

"Ultimately, a sustainable resolution to this geopolitical impasse cannot be achieved through fragmented, bilateral pacts driven by short-term expediency; rather, it must be consolidated formally **under the aegis of** the United Nations Security Council. Furthermore, the established hegemonic powers must internalize a modern, progressive sense of **noblesse oblige**, recognizing that their structural privileges within the global order inextricably bind them to the defense and underwriting of international law. Should they fail to assume this mantle of leadership, the resulting systemic vacuum will inevitably consume the very foundations of diplomatic stability."

**Analytical Insight for the Candidate:** Concluding the essay with the phrase **under the aegis of** solidifies the candidate's intellectual commitment to multilateralism, which is a core, unshakeable tenet of Brazilian foreign policy and MRE institutional culture. The integration of **noblesse oblige** provides a sophisticated, sociologically informed critique of great power responsibility. It demonstrates that the candidate views international relations not just as a mechanical interaction of states, but as a system governed by implicit moral and structural duties, fulfilling the ultimate criteria for stylistic mastery and lexical density required by Cebraspe.
